Introduction ¶

Dataset Source: ¶

Manu Siddhartha. (2020). Heart Disease Dataset (Comprehensive). IEEE Dataport. https://dx.doi.org/10.21227/dz4t-cm36

Dataset Details: ¶

The Heart Disease dataset is a comprehensive dataset comprised of five individual data sources: Cleveland, Hungarian, Switzerland, Long Beach VA and 'Stat-log' (Heart) datasets. These datasets are combined over 12 common features, with 11 of those being factors that can contribute to heart disease and one being whether or not the person suffers from heart disease. The combined dataset resulted in 1,190 rows and 12 columns of data.

Dataset Features: ¶

A summary of features contained in the dataset:

Feature Data Type Units Description
Age Numeric Years Age in Years
Sex Binary 0,1 Gender: 1 = Male, 0 = Female
Chest Pain Type Nominal 1,2,3,4 Value 1: typical angina, Value 2: atypical angina, Value 3: non-anginal pain and Value 4: asymptomatic
Resting blood pressure Numeric mm Hg Resting Blood Pressure in mm Hg
Serum Cholesterol Numeric mg/dl Serum Cholesterol in mg/dl
Fasting Blood Sugar Binary (fasting blood sugar > 120 mg/dl) (1 = true; 0 = false) Fasting Blood Sugar level greater than 120 mg/dl
Resting Electrocardiogram Results Nominal 0,1,2 Value 0: normal, Value 1: Having ST-T wave abnormality (T wave inversions and/or ST elevation or depression of > 0.05 mV), Value 2: showing probable or definite left ventricular hypertrophy by Estes' criteria
Maximum Heart Rate Achieved Numeric Beats Per Minute (bpm) Maximum Heart Rate as measured in bpm
Exercise Induced Angina Binary 0,1 1 = yes; 0 = no
oldpeak =ST Numeric Millimeters ST depression induced by exercise relative to rest, a measure of abnormality in electrocardiograms
ST Slope Nominal 1,2,3 The slope of the peak exercise ST segment, Value 1: up-sloping, Value 2: flat, Value 3: down-sloping
Target Binary 0,1 1 = Heart Disease, 0 = Normal

Target Feature: ¶

The target response feature for this dataset is labelled 'Target' and indicates the presence or absence of heart disease in the patient, where 1 = Heart Disease and 0 = Normal or the absence of Heart Disease.

Goals and Objectives ¶

The goal for Phase 1 of this assignment is to prepare the Heart Disease dataset for predictive modeling using Machine Learning techniques in Phase 2. The ultimate goal is to determine whether or not a patient will have heart disease given a set of independent variables.

The key objectives for this report include:

  • Exploring and visualising the variables within the dataset and examining relationships between the variables.

Removing Zero Values from Cholesterol, Resting Blood Pressure, and ST Slope ¶

Upon examining the descriptive statistics of the variables in this dataset minimum values of zero were seen for both cholesterol and resting blood pressure. This stood out as a person's cholesterol and resting blood pressure cannot be zero. Furthermore, a value of zero was seen in ST Slope which is an error as the categories for ST Slope are 1, 2, 3. Therefore, these values are treated as missing values and are removed. To confirm the values were correctly removed the shape and descriptive statistics were re-examined showing 173 zero values were removed from these variables.

Checking for Outliers ¶

To check for the presence of outliers in the dataset the mean, standard deviation, and maximum values of all numeric variables were examined. It was found that multiple variables had maximum values that were a lot larger than the mean suggesting the presence of outliers. To check and remove outliers the method of z-score greater than 3 was employed. The number of these values were checked and it was found that 39 rows of data had z-scores greater than 3. Therefore, these rows were removed using the below code (Sharma, 2018).

z = np.abs(stats.zscore(heart))

For cholesterol and resting blood pressure there was a small difference in the means of those with heart disease compared to those without heart disease. As expected in the Age box plot those that had heart disease were, on average, older than those without heart disease. However, unexpectedly in the Maximum Heart Rate box plot those with heart disease exhibited, on average, a lower maximum heart rate than those without heart disease. Multi-Variable Box Plots ¶

After seeing the large difference in the means of oldpeak by the target variable, oldpeak was analysed further. Analysing the multi-variable box plots, it is clear that the median oldpeak measurement, an indicator for poor heart health (i.e., ischemia) is higher in the heart disease group for patients with prior history of exercise angina and an ST slope of 3.

An ST slope of 3 indicates a down-sloping ST segment on an electrocardiogram (ECG) which is a common indicator of severe myocardial ischemia.

Conclusion ¶

The purpose of Phase 1 was to prepare and explore the Heart Disease dataset for machine learning. In order to do so, we needed to develop a strong understanding of the data, the existing relationships between variables and the key features related to the target variable.

From our visualisation and exploration of the data, we identified a number of key variables related to the presence of heart disease. This included age, oldpeak, ST Slope, exercise angina and chest pain type. Surprisingly, variables such as cholesterol levels and resting blood pressure did not indicate any difference between patients with or without heart disease.

The key takeaway from our analysis is that old age, higher oldpeak measurements, presence of exercise angina, asymptomatic chest pain and an ST Slope reading of 2 (flat) or 3 (downward sloping) was more pronounced in patients with heart disease compared to patients without heart disease.

Looking forward to Phase 2, some of the aforementioned variables identified will be instrumental for feature selection purposes.
